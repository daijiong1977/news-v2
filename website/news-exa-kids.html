Gaza Votes! First Election in 21 Years Brings Hope for Change

Imagine not having a say in who runs your town for more than 20 years. That’s how long it had been for people in Deir el-Balah, a city in Gaza. But on April 25, 2026, something amazing happened: they held their first municipal election in 21 years!

A municipal election is when people vote for who will lead their local government—the people in charge of things like clean water, electricity, and fixing roads. About 70,000 people over age 18 were allowed to vote. They could cast their ballots from 7 a.m. to 5 p.m. at 12 voting centers set up in places like stadiums and women’s activity centers.

This election was a big deal because for the past 21 years, the group Hamas had been in charge without any elections. Now, residents wanted a change. “The citizen today is not looking for slogans, but for real solutions,” said Rabha al-Bhaisi, a local resident. She meant people are tired of fancy promises—they want clean water, electricity that works, and good sewage systems.

Four groups of candidates competed for seats on the local council. Their names were Peace and Construction, Deir el-Balah Brings Us Together, Future of Deir el-Balah, and Renaissance of Deir el-Balah. None of them were from big political parties like Hamas or Fatah. Instead, they were independent groups made up of local people, including tribal leaders and professionals. Each list had at least 15 candidates, and at least four had to be women.

Voters didn’t just pick one list. They chose a list, then picked five individual candidates from that list. This system is called a “closed party list.” It helps make sure the council represents many different voices.

The election was run by the Palestinian Central Elections Commission (CEC), an independent group that makes sure votes are fair. Jamil al-Khalidi, the CEC’s regional director, said this vote was part of a bigger plan that includes 420 local councils in the West Bank. But Deir el-Balah was the only place in Gaza holding an election this time.

Why did they choose Deir el-Balah? Because it had less damage from the recent war than other parts of Gaza. Still, the city suffered a lot. The municipal building was destroyed, and the former mayor was killed in 2024. So this election was also about rebuilding hope.

Candidates focused on practical solutions. Abu Nasser, head of the Peace and Construction list, said they need “practical, innovative solutions with a strong reliance on young people.” Faten Harb, a candidate from another list, promised to be transparent—meaning honest and open about how decisions are made.

Some people wondered if the election would really make a difference. Salem Abu Hassanein, a media director for one list, said, “The real bet is on producing a council capable of serving people, away from narrow political calculations.” He meant the goal is to help everyone, not just certain groups.

This election also matters because it shows that democracy—letting people vote for their leaders—can happen even in tough times. As one observer noted, the vote could be “the beginning of a gradual return to democracy” or just a small step. But for now, the people of Deir el-Balah have spoken.

Why it matters: This election shows that even after hard times, people can use their voice to ask for change. It reminds us that voting is a powerful way to make your community better, and that every vote counts.

Ceasefire Confusion: Why Trump Extended the Deadline with Iran

Imagine you’re playing a game of tug-of-war, and just when the rope is about to snap, someone yells, “Wait, let’s take a break!” That’s kind of what happened between the United States and Iran this week.

President Trump extended a ceasefire deadline with Iran. A ceasefire is a pause in fighting so both sides can try to make peace. But this pause has been full of twists and turns.

Just hours before the ceasefire was set to end, Trump announced he was extending it indefinitely. That means no set end date. He said the Iranian government is “seriously fractured,” or split apart, and that talks would continue “one way or the other.”

But here’s the tricky part: even though Trump extended the ceasefire, the U.S. military is staying put. And the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports continues. A blockade is when one country uses ships to block another country’s ports so no ships can get in or out. Iran says that’s an act of war.

On the same day, Iran attacked and seized two ships in the Strait of Hormuz. The Strait of Hormuz is a narrow waterway that’s super important for oil tankers and other ships. Iran has been blocking ships from passing through. A third ship was hit, according to Iranian news.

Iran’s Revolutionary Guard Corps said the attacks were a response to the U.S. blockade. Iran wants the U.S. to end its blockade before it will agree to peace talks.

So why did Trump extend the ceasefire? Pakistan is helping to mediate, which means they’re like a referee trying to get both sides to talk. Pakistan asked Trump to hold off on more attacks. Trump agreed.

But not everyone is happy. An Iranian adviser said the extension “means nothing.” He accused the U.S. of using the extension as a trick to buy time for a surprise attack. The U.S. says that’s not true.

Meanwhile, Vice President Vance was supposed to travel to Pakistan for peace talks, but Iran refused to meet because of the blockade. Iran’s nuclear capabilities are also a big concern, but right now the main fight is over who controls the Strait of Hormuz.

Before the war, ships could pass freely through the strait. Now they can’t. That’s a big problem for countries around the world that depend on oil and goods that travel through there.

So what happens next? No one knows for sure. Trump has been going back and forth between tough threats and offers to talk. Iran is watching closely. The ceasefire is extended, but the tension is still high.

Why it matters: This story matters because the Strait of Hormuz is a highway for oil and goods that people all over the world use. When countries fight over it, prices can go up, and peace can feel far away. Understanding how leaders try to solve big problems—sometimes by talking, sometimes by threatening—helps you see how the world works.

Health Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. Faces Tough Questions from Senators

Imagine being asked tough questions by a room full of senators for hours. That’s exactly what happened to Robert F. Kennedy Jr., the new head of the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS). He testified before two Senate committees on Wednesday, facing questions about vaccines, drug prices, and more.

Kennedy’s job is to help keep Americans healthy. He leads HHS, which oversees things like food safety, medicine, and disease prevention. This was his first time testifying before Congress since September, and senators from both political parties had a lot to ask.

Some Republican senators praised Kennedy for his work on rural health and nutrition. They liked his efforts to encourage healthy eating and phase out certain artificial food dyes. But Democratic senators were more critical. They pushed him on several topics, including vaccine policy and the cost of promotional videos from his office.

One big topic was vaccines. Kennedy made changes to the childhood vaccine schedule without getting advice from outside experts. This means he changed which vaccines kids should get and when. Some senators were worried about this because the U.S. has seen more measles cases in 2025 than in the last 30 years. Measles is a serious disease that vaccines can prevent.

Kennedy also talked about his agency’s wins. He mentioned negotiating lower drug prices, releasing new Dietary Guidelines, expanding nutrition education in medical schools, and creating a Rural Health Transformation Fund to help people in the countryside get better healthcare.

Another topic was TrumpRx, a new website where people can find discounts on some prescription drugs. Senator Elizabeth Warren asked why some drugs on the site cost more than prices available elsewhere. Kennedy defended the program.

Senator Maggie Hassan questioned Kennedy about a pesticide called glyphosate. Before joining the government, Kennedy had sued the company that makes it, saying it was harmful. But President Trump signed an order to increase production of the pesticide. Kennedy said the order was needed for national security and that the president gave him $200 million to help reduce the country’s use of glyphosate.

Throughout the hearings, Kennedy defended his health agenda and his agency’s budget request for 2027. He said HHS has “delivered historic wins” in its first 15 months under his leadership.

These hearings were part of a busy week for Kennedy. He testified before seven different committees in seven days. Senators will keep watching his work closely as he leads HHS.

Why it matters: This story matters because the decisions made by the Health Secretary affect your health—from the vaccines you get to the food you eat and the medicines your family might need. Understanding how leaders are questioned helps you see how our government works to keep people safe.
