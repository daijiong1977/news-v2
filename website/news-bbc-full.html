Surprise! Rare Mexican Axolotl Found in Welsh River

Imagine you're splashing in a shallow river on a family trip, and you spot a strange, smiling creature with feathery gills hiding among the rocks. That's exactly what happened to 12-year-old Evie in the River Ogmore in Bridgend, Wales. She had discovered a Mexican axolotl — a critically endangered salamander that almost never appears in the wild outside of Mexico. Experts say this is the first time an axolotl has ever been documented living wild in the UK.

Evie and her family were so amazed that they cut their trip short to bring the animal home to Leicester. They named it Dippy, as a tribute to the spot where it was found. "I think it's a really nice change to the family," Evie said. But how did a creature from Mexico end up in a Welsh river? Chris Newman, director of the National Centre for Reptile Welfare, believes Dippy was someone's pet that got released when its owner's "circumstances changed." That's a big problem: axolotls aren't native to the UK, and releasing a pet into the wild can be dangerous for both the animal and the local environment.

Axolotls are famous for their incredible ability to regrow lost body parts, like limbs or even parts of their brain. In the wild, they live only in a few lakes near Mexico City. But pollution and habitat loss have made them critically endangered — scientists estimate only 50 to 1,000 remain in the wild. That's fewer than the number of pandas! In captivity, though, they're popular pets and research animals.

Some people think finding Dippy is exciting and a great chance to learn about a rare species. Others worry that releasing exotic pets into rivers could harm local wildlife. Conservationists say it's crucial never to dump a pet outdoors; instead, contact a rescue center. Evie's family did the right thing by taking Dippy home, and now the little axolotl has a safe place to live.
